## 1 GENERAL

This is a super-alpha titanium alloy having an alpha-stabilized Ti-Al matrix solid solution strengthened by the additions of tin and zirconium. The beta-stabilizing addition, molybdenum, increases room and elevated temperature tensile strength and stability. The alloy is used primarily in jet engine compressor parts and airframe skin components.

The alloy possesses good strength properties up to 1000 F, exceeding Ti-8Al-1Mo-1V and, for the annealed condition, Ti-6Al-4V. On a strength-to-weight basis, it is also superior to Ti-679. Its creep properties are better than Ti-8Al-1Mo-1V, Ti-6Al-4V and, above 900F, Ti-679. The alloy is more susceptible to solid-salt stress corrosion than Ti-679 but less susceptible than Ti-6Al-4V, Ti-8Al-1Mo-1V and Ti-5Al-2.5Sn in the range of commercial mill products and heat treatments. At elevated temperatures, the alloy is more fatigue resistant than Ti-8Al-1Mo-1V and Ti-679. The alloy appears metallurgically stable up to 800 F. Forgeability of the alloy is judged similar to Ti-8Al-1Mo-1V and Ti-6Al-4V. Weld properties of the alloy are variable.

Producers (78, 79) give assurances that silicon segregation in the modified alloy is no problem with modern melting practice. That the high solubility of silicon in the alloy (due principally to the alloy's molybdenum content) precludes precipitation of a silicide compound during heat treatment or thermal exposure. It is considered essential (79) that the silicon-modified alloy is beta processed if improvements in creep resistance are to be assured.



Creep resistance and thermal stability of the silicon-modified alloy are enhanced by solution treating high in the alpha-beat field (commonly at beta transus less 25 F). These effects are illustrated in Figures 1.0426 and 1.0427 for rolled bar and forged upsets. The rolled bar primary-alpha grains were small, equiaxed, non-continuous and non-aligned, surrounded by aged metastable beta products. Its thermal stability was unaffected over the range of solution temperatures from 80 to 25 F below the beta transus. The forged upsets had large, elongation, aligned and continuous primary-alpha grains, which are damaging to creep resistance and thermal stability (78). High-temperature solution treatment reduces the amount of primary alpha as shown in Figure 1.0428 (and hence the amount of continuous primary alpha), and broadens elongated grains so that they tend to become equiaxed and alignment is diminished. This improves creep resistant (Figure 1.0426) and metallurgical stability, as expressed in Figure 1.0427 most notably by a rise in post-exposure reduction or area for the forged upsets.

#### Heat Treatment

General. This alloy is used in either a duplex- or a triplex-annealed condition. The first cycle of either treatment is a solution anneal (1650 F, alpha solution anneal, or 1750 F, alpha + beta solution anneal). The subsequent 1450 F heating cycle for sheet is a stabilization anneal having little or no effect on room temperature tensile properties. The final 1100 F heating cycle for triplex-annealed sheet and duplex-annealed bar and forgings produces a modest improvement in room temperature tensile strength indicating some aging takes place; this treatment is sometimes referred to as a stabilization age.

#### Anneal.

1.0521 Sheet. Duplex: 1650 F, ½ hour, air cool + 1450 F ¼ hour, air cool. Triplex: 1650 F, ½ hour, air cool + 1450 F, ¼ hour, air cool + 1100 F, 2 hours, air cool. The second cycle of either treatment is designed to be compatible with forming operations such as hot sizing. The additional 1100 F, 2 hours treatment is also compatible with certain hot forming operations or may be added as an aging treatment following the hot sizing operation if higher uniaxial strength at room and elevated temperatures is desired. This triplex treatment has little, if any, effect on sharp-notch tensile strength and

produces superior creep resistance and smooth tensile strength stability. Sharp notch strength stability without load is extended to higher exposure temperature by triplex-annealing, but embrittlement is more pronounced at those temperatures where embrittlement occurs. The triplex treatment is damaging to sustained load failure characteristics in salt-water environment (see Table 2.0331).

Bar and forgings. Less than 2½-inch section: 1750 F, 1 hour, air cool + 1100 F, 8 hours, air cool. Greater than 2½-inch section: 1650 F, 1 hour, air cool + 1100 F, 8 hours, air cool; or 1750 F, 1 hour, air cool + 1100 F, 8 hours, air cool.

The 1650 F solution anneal with an 1100 F, 8 hour stabilization age provides somewhat higher tensile strengths at room and elevated temperatures and fatigue strength as good or better than that for the higher solution anneal temperature (1750 F). The higher solution anneal with the same stabilization age provides superior creep resistance, but no demonstrated improvement in smooth tensile strength stability. Room temperature notch stress rupture strength is somewhat improved, as is the Charpy V impact energy at room temperature and -80 F.

Solution annealing at 1790 F (beta transus less 25 F) can further improve creep resistance, but not consistently. However, its potential benefit warrants its use. As discussed in Section 1.042, solution treating at 1790 F in combination with 0.08 to 0.1 weight-percent silicon addition and beta processing substantially improves creep resistance and stability. The higher solution treatment for the standard composition benefits room and elevated temperature strength, room temperature plane strain fracture toughness, room temperature and -80 F Charpy V impact energy and room temperature mild-notch stress rupture life. Solution treating above the beta transus is not beneficial.

Faster quenching from the solution temperature in media other than air improves tensile strength but with a sacrifice in creep resistance.

### 1.06 Hardness

Stabilization aging at 1100 F raises the solution annealed tensile strength of this alloy about 10 ksi indicating only slight hardenability. Sections up to 3 inches, the recommended duplex condition, exhibit uniform tensile properties throughout; however, water quenching from the solution anneal treatment followed by a stabilization age produces a 15 ksi strength differential from center to outside. Thus, the alloy has hardening characteristics about like Ti-6Al-4V (7).

### 1.08 Melting and Casting Practice

1.081 Wrought products. Multiple melted using consumable-electrode vacuum practice. Standard quality, double melted. Premium quality: triple melted.

1.082 Castings. Two recent programs [Air Force Contracts F33(615)-70-C-1409 with TRW, Inc. and F33(615)-70-1410 with REM Metals Corp.] have been carried out to study various aspects of casting this alloy, including foundry variables, alloy chemistry and heat treatment effects, casting defects and cast properties. The programs are distinguished principally by the melting methods used: induction melting by TRW and consumable electrode skull melting by REM. The two programs are discussed separately below according to the melting practice employed.

1.083 Induction melting. Chemical interaction between molten alloy and crucible has been a major deterrent to induction melting titanium-base alloys. TRW, however, has recently developed a semi-levitation induction meting process, which purportedly allows for good control of power input and limits the amount of crucible contamination by making use of levitation forces generated during induction meting (54). Graphite crucibles are used, with minimized molten/crucible contact time, and direct pour into the mold. Melt temperature is uniform and constant during pouring, and fixed for a given setup. Unlike consumable electrode melting, superheating is possible with induction melting, an important feature considering titanium alloys' limited solidification range. Molten metal temperature is measure using infrared pyrometers which have rapid response and, unlike optical pyrometers operating in the visible portion of the spectrum, are relatively insensitive to emissivity changes with temperature.



Using this melting technique, castings were made to determine the castability (fluidity) of this alloy and the mechanical properties of the cast metal. Pyrolytic graphite faced silicate bonded molds produced by a patented process (55) were used. Castability castings weighted six pounds and consisted of two clusters of four bars each. Bar sizes were 0.75-inch wide x 4-inch long x 0.030-, 0.060-, 0.090- and 0.250-inch thick. One cluster was gated at both top and bottom; the other gated only at the bottom. Two pour temperatures were used: low superheat (liquidus + 100-200 F) and high superheat (liquidus + 200-350 F). Liquid metal temperature was determined in the pour cup using tungsten-rhenium thermocouples in molybdenum tubes. Mold preheat temperatures of 1000, 1500 and 2000 F were investigated. The effects of these casting variables on mold fill are presented in Table 1.085. These results reveal difficulty in filling sections as thin as 0.060 inch. Filling was complete for the heavier gages, but radiographic analyses revealed internal defects: 0.090-inch sections were porous and 0.250-inch sections contained microshrink or slight porosity.

Six-pound tensile bar cluster castings were made to determine the room temperature and 600 F tensile properties of the cast metal. Each casting contained six standard 0.250-inch diameter test specimens, three bottom gated and three top and bottom gated. The same combinations of pour and mold preheat temperatures used in the castability determinations were used for these castings. Complete mold fill was achieved in every case; however, some minor surface reaction was evident on all test bars. Companion specimens of Ti-6Al-4V alloy had 0.0001-inch surface reaction layers of TiC, which caused marked reductions in conventional tensile ductility. On the basis on this experience, test bars of the present alloy were sandblasted to remove 0.001 inch of the test section surface prior to testing. Test results for selected specimens are presented in Table 1.086. The castings were not completely sound, showing some evidence of centerline microshrink or slight porosity. These defects appear to have no effect on the strength properties of the cast alloy, but may be presumed responsible for the observed scatter in ductility values.

Production of a shaped casting was attempted using a quarter section of the #2 bearing housing of the General Electric TF-39 engine. Basting weight was 10 pounds; minimum casting wall thickness was 0.125 inch. Castings were poured using the same techniques used for the castability and tensile bar castings. In all instances, complete mold fill was achieved; however, the quality of the castings produced was generally disappointing. Particular problems were surface defects and porosity. Surface defects were similar to those encountered in more conventional casting processes: hot tears caused by mold cracking during solidification; cold shuts resulting from the use of too cold a mold; fold-type defects produced by titanium carbide formation on the surface of the liquid as it entered the mold and, due to stream turbulence, folding at the casting surface; inclusions caused by spalling of the pyrolytic graphite mold coating; and rattails resulting from entrapment of zirconium silicate mold material disrupted by the molten metal.

The gas porosity encountered in these casting trials is common in the casting of titanium-base alloy, arising in part from the large difference in gas solubility between liquid and solid. Pouring under vacuum magnifies the effect, as does the lightness of titanium, which keeps it from developing a high metallostatic head. In the present case, gas porosity occurred randomly throughout the castings and efforts to eliminate it by gating and venting modifications of the casting were unsuccessful. Void pocket analyses identified the entrapped gas as hydrogen. The use of degassed charge materials and vacuum degassing the molds and crucibles prior to pouring had no appreciable effect in reducing porosity. It was concluded that the only effective means for eliminating gas pores would be the use of massive gating systems for static casting, or resort to centrifugal or other non-static forms of

Conventional mechanical properties were determined on specimens extracted from the sidewalls of the bearing castings. Little difference was found from the properties of cast tensile bars, except for reduced ductility associated with gas porosity and/or massive alpha grain microstructures. Weld repairability was cursorily investigated with the result that, using the maximum bend angle to fracture the three-point loading as criterion, TIG welding reduced the ductility of the cast product.

General problems with the used of graphite crucibles and molds were pervasive in the attempt to produce acceptable castings by the induction melting technique. Surface reaction with the mold, as indicated by the titanium carbide surface layer, subsurface carbide network and alpha case, was prevalent and appeared to increase with increasing melt superheat and mold preheat temperature (see Table 1.087). Carbon pickup during melting due to crucible dissolution was appreciable and independent of superheat. Determinations on castings poured into steel molds showed increases from an initial value of 0.02 weight-percent to between 0.10 and 0.15 weight-percent. These values not only exceed present allowables for wrought stock, but also have an unfavorable effect on castability (see Figure 1.088). Re-melting further increased carbon pick-up, but less than during initial melting (see Figure 1.089). On the basis of these results (using 6 to 10 pound charges), it appears that carbon pickup associated with this melting process cannot be reliably controlled to currently accepted levels. The prospect of reclaiming sprues, gates, scrap castings, etc., is dim. No changes were found in the oxygen or nitrogen levels during melting. As expected for vacuum melting processes, hydrogen levels dropped from the as-received melt stock levels.



### 1.084 Consumable electrode melting.

The problem of molten metal reaction with the furnace crucible is purportedly circumvented by consumable electrode vacuum "skull" melting in water cooled copper crucibles (56). A skin of solid titanium, called a "skull", is formed which provides a non-reactive wall between the molten titanium and the copper crucible. This melting method is used by REM Metals Corporation.

The basic operation of skull melting is identical to conventional consumable arc melting, except the arc power (current) requirements are about twice that of conventional arc melting. This is so that a large molten pool forms rapidly and that a minimum of alloy solidified before the melt is poured. The size of the molten pool will depend on melt rate, crucible size and electrode configuration. When a sufficient pool is formed, the power is cut, the electrode stub withdrawn and the charge poured into the mold by titling the copper crucible. The complete sequence takes only a matter of minutes, with the casting portion taking only seconds. The time from electrode withdrawal to the completion of pour is very critical due to the narrow solidification range of most titanium alloys. Following pouring, the furnace is back-filled with a partial pressure of argon to promote cooling. When the mold has cooled sufficiently to avert harmful reaction with air, the furnace is opened and the mold removed.

The temperature of the melt is a continuous gradient from the hottest region (near the arc) to the coldest (near the crucible wall). No attempt is made to determine melt temperature before pouring, but pour conditions are replicated by controlling power input and coolant flow rate.

All castings produced by REM in their program were by their patented "REMET" investment casting process using tungsten lined ceramic molds preheated to 700 F (except when mold preheat was an experimental variable). Fluidity was determined as the ability to fill either 0.030-inch or 0.060-ionch thick "wings" appended to combination tensile bar/Charpy bar cluster castings. Like TRW's experience, the results indicated difficulty in filling sections as thin as 0.060 inch.

Four foundry practice variables were studied: mold preheat temperature, electrode size, crucible size and melt weight. Each of these factors could be expected to influence metal temperature, or heat content, and thereby affect surface finish and internal soundness and, consequently, mechanical properties. Triplicate variable-thickness, step-plate castings were poured for each foundry variable. Throughout, power input, cooling water flow rate, pour speed and mold configurations (except for a slightly larger pouring cup when enlarged melt size was studied) were held constant. Castings were heat-treated (1650 F, 1 hour, HeC + 1100 F, 8 hours, HeC) in full section prior to sectioning for a smooth tensile specimens.

Casting quality as influenced by foundry practice is documented in Table 1.0810. Despite the inability to superheat by consumable electrode melting (increasing arc power merely increases electrode consumption rate) and the alloy's limited solidification range, all castings were completely filled. However, some evident of cold metal was observed as lines (cold laps to cold shuts) or circles (cold shot) on casting surfaces. Most of these surface imperfections were slight, many requiring directional lighting to even be seen. This accounts for the disparity between the reported visual and fluorescent penetrate surface imperfection indications in Table 1.0810. The "reference" and "hot mold" conditions gave the least number of indications and the least internal shrinkage (porosity) in the thinnest cast sections. All other changes in foundry practice reduced casting quality by these measures. Overall, dimensional shrinkage was the only quality index unaffected by variations in foundry practice, the uniformity being well within the normal casting tolerance of ± 0.005inch/inch.

Room temperature smooth tensile properties were general unaffected by variations in foundry practice variables, except that in a few cases thin section shrinkage markedly reduced ductility. Grain size for the step castings rose smoothly from the thinnest to the thickest section. Average grain diameter was unaffected by foundry variables, as were macrostructure and micro-hardness. An attempt was made, using micro-hardness measurements, to determine the extent of possible casting surface reaction with the mold or other processing environments. Average hardness was essentially uniform from about 0.002 inch to as much as 0.250 inch below the casting surface. Scatter in the data was considerable: however, no attempt was made to identify its source (i.e., compositional or microstructural differences or both). It should be recalled that TRW observed metallographic indications of surface reaction by their casting process at depths nearer the surface than explored here and that such a possibility cannot be ruled out here. The conventional smooth tensile properties of stabilization aged bars (1100 F, 8 hours, HeC) with and without test section machining indicated. However, no evidence of surface reaction as regards its influence on these properties.



The separate influences of oxygen and iron contents on the stabilization aged mechanical properties of cast bars are presented in Figures 1.0811 through 1.0818. Increasing oxygen has the anticipated effect of increasing strength and reducing ductility. Toughness and mild-notch creep rupture strength. Increasing iron had no practical effect over the range studied.

An important consideration in the usefulness of cast products is weldability. Welding is essential in removing harmful defects, affecting the economics of production particularly as the casting size and complexity increase, and in the ability to join cast parts to mating components. Weld reparability was determined by comparing unwelded cast specimens with those whose test cross section was 50 percent TIG repair weld. Results, for specimens with and without post-weld heat treatment, are given in Table 1.0819. The elongation of repair welded samples without post weld heat treatment and those alpha solution annealed plus stabilization aged (1650 F, 1 hour, HeC + 1100 F, 8 hours, HeC) were below companion unwelded values. Isothermal treatment of 1900 F, 1 hour + 1650 F, 1 hour + 1100 F, 4 hours, HeC restored repair weld ductility, but at a reduced strength level for both welded and unwelded specimens (compared to as-cast values). Beta solution treating plus aging (1900 F, 1 hour, HeC + 1100 F, 4 hours, HeC) produces strengths and ductilities for repair welded and unwelded specimens equal to each other and to the as-cast values. It is claimed that this treatment produces microstructural uniformity throughout the repair-welded stock. However, not all mechanical properties benefit from this heat treatment, as do the smooth tensile properties. Charpy impact energy, 100-hour creep strain at 1000 F, 35 ksi, and post-creep tensile properties were all equivalent for

repair welded and unwelded specimens given the beta solution annealed plus age treatment; but repair weld damage to the room temperature step-loaded mild notch creep rupture and smooth axial fatigue strengths was not remedied by the heat treatment.

Difficulty was experienced in weld joint thin-to-thin and thin-to-thick sections; lack of penetration and/or severe porosity was particularly damaging to ductility, and the situation was only partially improved by modifications in welding technique. Problems with welding this alloy, discussed in Section 4.03, cast doubt on the investigators' claim that further improvements in technique will eventually produce acceptable weld joints.

The effects of heat treatment on the mechanical properties of the cast product are presented in Tables 1.0820 through 1.0823. Test specimens were machined from cast-oversize bars following heat treatment. The smooth tensile properties of bars stabilization aged 1100 F, 8 hours, AC (Table 1.0820) are in agreement with those determined in the TRW program for induction melted cast bars similarly aged. Prior alpha-beta solutioning at 1750 F has no effect on aged strength, but produces considerable scatter in ductility (not withstanding the possibility of variable cast bar quality). Other mechanical properties reported in Tables 1.0821 through 1.0823 appear either unaffected or somewhat improved by the alpha-beta solution treatment preceding aging.

In an attempt to produce a more uniform structure (alpha precipitate) than was initially established on cooling the newly frozen alloy, a beta solution treatment at 1900 F was applied to two groups of specimens. This treatment requires less careful control of the temperature than is required for alpha-beta solutioning, where small variations can produce large differences in aged properties. Water quenching form the beta solution temperature, and subsequent aging, produces substantial strengthening but has a calamitous effect on ductility and those properties reported in Tables 1.0821 through 1.0823. Slower cooling from the solution treatment followed by an abbreviated age produces properties little different from those obtained by simple stabilization aging except to scatter the results a bit more. This latter treatment, however, is considered necessary for repair welds as described previously concerning weld repair studies. Consequently, the mechanical properties reported in the balance of this section on castings (Figures and Tables 1.0824 through 1.0828) are for this heat-treated condition.

To check the suitability of this casting technique to actual hardware, production of several C-5A cargo tie-down rings was attempted. Casing quality was judged satisfactory by the producer and smooth tensile properties matched those of cast-to-size bars and bars extracted from step castings.

### 1.09 Special Considerations

Stability. General. For many high-temperature applications, the usefulness of a particular alloy is limited by its elevated temperature stability. This property is commonly evaluated from room temperature tensile tests on specimens previously exposed to elevated temperatures with and without load. Because of the importance of this property for critical applications, a large body of such information has been accumulated for Ti-6Al-2Sn-4Zr-2Mo alloy. The effect of exposure on room temperature smooth tensile properties for sheet, bar and forgings in the whole range of commercial sizes are presented in Figures and Tables 3.02114 through 3.02132. Exposure effects on room temperature notch tensile properties of sheet are presented in Figures 3.02718 through 3.027111. Limited data on the effect of exposure on the room temperature shear strength of sheet are presented in Figure 3.0251. The influence of forging and solution-annealed temperatures on the elevated temperature stability of forgings is given in Tables 4.0121 and 4.0122. A single set of data on the effect of elevated temperature exposure on the smooth tensile properties of sheet, at the exposure temperature, is presented in Figure 3.03126.

The extent to which room temperature properties will be affected by previous exposure will depend on the form and condition of the products as well as the time, temperature and stress level of exposure. While the available data are extensive, they are not systematic and no firm generalizations can be made at this time. However, the following conclusions appear to be supported by the present results.



Stability – 150 hours. For sheet in both the duplex-and triplex-annealed conditions, 150-hour exposure at 1000 F, 25 ksi produces no deleterious effect on subsequent room temperature tensile properties. This is also true for exposure at lower temperatures and higher stress levels; for example, 900 F at 45 ksi and 800 F at 65 ksi (Figure 3.02116, Table 3.02117 and Figure 3.02119). Likewise, 100-hour exposure to 1000 F without load produces no loss in room temperature sharp notch tensile strength of either duplex- or triplex-annealed sheet (Figures 3.02118 and 3.02110).

Duplex-annealed rolled bar may or may not be affected by exposures of 150 hours with load at temperatures up to 1000 F depending on the exposure stress. At 25-ksi stress level, no practical loss in room temperature smooth tensile properties is observed (Table 3.02121). While at 30 ksi, a sensible loss in ductility is observed (Figure 3.02120). Bar rolled in the beta region is unaffected by 150-hour exposure at 1000 F, 30 ksi (Table 3.02124).

Forged bar in the recommended duplex-annealed condition exposure 150 hours up to 1000 F, 25 ksi exhibits no loss in subsequent room temperature smooth tensile properties (Figure 3.02126 and Table 3.02127). Contrasting results were obtained from forged compressor wheels (Tables 3.02128 and 3.02129). The hub location in one sheet exposed at 800 F, 70 ksi, 150 hours showed no losses in elongation and reductions of area (Table 3.02128). Other locations in the same forging, however, were essentially unaffected. A second wheel forging, water quenched from the solution anneal, showed losses in ductility after exposures at 900 F/55 ksi, 1000 F/35 ksi or 1100 F/15 ksi (Table 3.02129).

Stability – long time. 1000-hour exposure up to 1000 F without load has no deleterious effect on subsequent room temperature tensile properties of duplex-annealed sheet (Figure 3.02115). In contrast, 1000-hour exposure with load produces a serious loss in ductility at 900 F, 20 ksi for duplex-annealed sheet and at 1000 F, 10 ksi for triplex-annealed sheet (Table 3.02118). At 950 F, 15 ksi no damage is done to triplex-annealed sheet for 1000-hour exposure (Table 3.02118). It appears from these limited results, that the triplex-annealed condition is more stable than the duplex condition. As expected, no change in tensile properties at the exposure temperatures is observed after 1000-hour exposure without load up to 1000 F and perhaps beyond (Figure 3.03126).

Figures 3.02718 to 3.027111 show the influence of exposure to elevated temperature without load on the subsequent room temperature sharp notch

strength of sheet. This property is a sensitive indicator of instability and reveals the onset of serious embrittlement at 900 F for 1000-hour exposure, for both duplex- and triplex-annealed conditions.

Bar in the recommended duplex-annealed condition exposed 1000 hours with load at temperatures up to 1000 F exhibits no significant instability (Table 3.02122). However, for longer exposure times a loss in room temperature tensile ductility is observed for lower exposure temperatures (Table 3.02123). Thus a marked reduction in ductility results from 3000-hours exposure at 825 F, 47.5 ksi.

Substantial losses in ductility are observed for forgings in the recommended duplex-annealed condition following 500-hour exposure at 800 F, 75 ksi (Table 3.02131). A single result for the same forgings showed no ductility loss for 100-hour exposure at 800 F, 80 ksi. However, additional forgings (Figure 3.02130) substantiate the onset of instability at exposure temperatures as low as 800 F as indicated by losses in reduction of area. These results (Figure 3.02130) were independent of specimen surface machining following exposure, indicating metallurgical changes rather than mere surface damage. The results of Figure 3.02130 also seem to contradict the suggestion (78, 79, 85) that solution annealing temperature approaching the beta transus temperature improve metallurgical stability. On the other hand, high solution temperatures (below the beta transus) are not damaging, and their beneficial effect on creep resistance and stability when combined with modest silicon additions warrants their use as discussed in Section 1.042. Solution annealed above the beta transus temperature is also ineffectual in improving thermal stability (Table 4.0121), as is beta forging followed by the recommended duplex-anneal treatment (Table 4.0122). As discussed in Section 1.042, however, beta processing in combination with added silicon is effective [indeed considered essential (79)] in improving creep resistance.

### 2.03 Chemical Properties

2.031 Corrosion resistance. General. In common with other titanium alloys, Ti-6Al-2Sn-4Zr-2Mo is susceptible to solid salt stress corrosion at elevated temperatures and exhibits delayed failure of cracked specimens at room temperature in aqueous salt environments.

2.032 Solid salt corrosion. Embrittlement and/or cracking of titanium alloys while stressed in salt environments at elevated temperatures is well known and commonly referred to as hot-salt stress corrosion. Measured salt deposits and operating stresses and temperatures of compressor components in current aircraft turbine engines are sufficient to cause failure by this mode in laboratory specimens, but no such service failures have been reported in the literature. It is presumed that the lack of correspondence between predicted behavior and service experience lies in a lack of correspondence between laboratory simulation and actual engine operation. Added to this, there is a distressing lack of test standardization that makes any prediction of service failure open to question.

A common test for hot-salt stress corrosion involves exposure of salt-coated specimens to elevated temperature under load (usually static tension) and subsequent visual examination for corrosion or cracking, and/or measurement of residual room temperature mechanical properties for indications of embrittlement. On a plot of exposure stress versus exposure temperature, threshold curves separate regions of cracking from no cracking, or embrittlement from non-embrittlement. Substantial variations in test procedure and stress corrosion criteria account for substantial differences in thresholds reported by different investigators.

A particularly interesting series of tests by Gray and Johnston (57, 58, 61, 62, 63, 64) has been directed toward the problems of hot-salt stress corrosion testing and to the disparity between laboratory and service experience. Most of the work was done on Ti-8Al-1Mo-1V alloy because of its known sensitivity to hot-salt stress corrosion and because of its early consideration for application in hot-salt environments. A substantial amount of information was also developed in those studies for Ti-6Al-2Sn-4Zr-2Mo alloy. The data are combined in what follows in a general discussion of hot-salt stress corrosion testing, with particular emphasis on the behavior of Ti-6Al-2Sn-4Zr-2Mo alloy.

In Gray and Johnston’s studies, tubular specimens were used in conjunction with a dynamic air apparatus, which simulated salt deposition and exposure conditions of actual aircraft turbine engines. The influences of both specimen preparation and specimen exposure conditions on hot-salt stress corrosion behavior were examined. Specimen preparation variables were residual machining stresses, salt deposition temperature and salt concentration. Exposure variables were airflow, air pressure, air dewpoint (humidity), salt-in-air concentration, temperature and stress. Post-exposure tensile tests were performed at a constant test machine crosshead speed to specimen failure of 0.005in/min. This loading was considered essential to the believed stress corrosion mechanism of hydrogen production, absorption, diffusion and embrittlement, observable only at temperatures at or slightly exceeding room temperature and at loading rates compatible with hydrogen diffusion rates (62). Stress corrosion thresholds were based on either cracking during exposure or on an arbitrarily assigned reduction in subsequently measured room temperature tensile ductility; elongation less than 15% and reduction of area (based on change in outside diameter) less than 25%. Cracking during exposure was evidenced by the presence on the fracture surface of regions covered with oxides and corrosion products. It is claimed that cracks as small as 0.001 inch were easily detected under X30 magnification. Specimen cylindrical surfaces were not inspected for damage, and the possibility of microcracks contributing to reduced ductility (embrittlement) was not explored. Specimens customarily were coated by exposing, without applied load, the inner bore for one hour to a salt-in-air concentration of 40 parts per billion at an air velocity of 1000 feet/second at 400 F and dewpoint of -120 F and 30 psia pressure. This elaborate procedure produced uniform deposits of 0.6 mg/in², which was within the range measure on service airfoils (59, 60). Higher deposition temperatures, or continuous deposition during test exposure, were examined only for mill-annealed Ti-8Al-1Mo-1V and were seen to raise the apparent embrittlement threshold (61): data for Ti-6Al-2Sn-4Zr-2Mo were not obtained.



Salt concentrations of 0.2 and 2.0 mg/in², bracketing the range measure on actual airfoils, produced substantially identical embrittlement thresholds for Ti-8Al-1Mo-1V alloy in Gray and Johnston's experiments (61). However, earlier studies (65, 66, 67) showed that light coatings were more damaging than heavy coatings (purportedly due to the greater availability of moisture and oxygen in the cast of the light coatings). Puffer et al (60) show cracking for Ti-6Al-2Sn-4Zr-2Mo increases continually with increasing salt concentration from very light to very heavy concentrations (0.5 to 50 mg/in²).

Compressive surface stresses can raise the stress corrosion threshold, sometimes substantially. For Ti-8Al-1Mo-1V, the embrittlement threshold, for as-machined specimens (presumably with residual

compressive machining stresses) exceeded that for specimens annealed either by heat treatment or by chemically milling (dissolving) the surface, by a factor of two. For Ti-6Al-2Sn-4Zr-2Mo mill-annealed bar, much smaller effects were observed, as shown for dynamic exposures in Figure 2.0324. Attempts to confirm the presence of residual machining stresses in these tests with x-ray analyses were unsuccessful, and the equally plausible effect of contamination and/or attack during heat treatment of chemically milling was not disposed of. However, the very substantial benefit from glass bead blasting compressor disc forgings shown in Figure 2.03212 seems to substantiate the presumption of residual compressive stresses in the previous work, and attests to the potentially significant benefit of same. One should be advised, however, that initial benefits from compressive surface stresses could disappear in service due to stress relief during exposure.

Actual engine environmental conditions are unique and considered less conducive to stress corrosion than laboratory exposure conditions. This in fact appears borne out by the lack of recorded service failures. Possible ameliorating engine conditions are high air velocities, high pressure, salt-air conditions, oil contamination and/or unique operating cycles. In the same relevant studies of Gray and Johnston, air velocity of 1100 ft/sec and air pressure increase from 30 to 52 psia were each determined to have only a minor effect on the embrittlement threshold of Ti-8Al-1Mo-1V alloy (61). Essentially no effect of air velocity is seen for the Ti-6Al-2Sn-4Zr-2Mo alloy in Figure 2.0324 (compare dynamic and static air exposures for chemically-milled specimens). Gray and Johnston report no systematic influence of exposure air moisture in the range -120 to +40 F dewpoint for Ti-8Al-1Mo-1V, but this is at variance with numerous experiments reported in the literature that show increased hot-salt stress corrosion with increasing moisture in the environment. As expected, increased exposure times universally produce increased indications of stress corrosion.

The influence of exposure cycling was studied for Ti-8Al-1Mo-1V (64). Temperature and stress were cycled separately and in combination, the latter simulating a service cycle of current aircraft turbine engine compressor components. Cyclic exposure in which notch temperature and stress were applied for 1 hour and removed for 3 hours, for a total of 96 hours at temperature, had associated crack threshold stresses twice those for continuous 96-hour exposure. The same was true for cycles of temperature, 1 hour on and 3 hours off, at constant stress. Other temperature cycles (3 hours on/3 hours off, 1 hour on/1 hour off and 3 hours on/1 hour off) at constant stress, and a stress cycle of 1 hour on and 3 hours off at constant temperature raised the continuous-exposure (for the same total time at temperature) threshold by 60 to 90% at the lowest exposure temperature, but these improvements disappeared as the exposure temperature increased. It has been suggested that temperature cycling interferes with the production and decomposition of the intermediate corrosion product titanium dichloride ( $ TiCl_{2} $), necessary for the production of the embrittling corrosion product, hydrogen. Stress cycling is thought to lessen the buildup of diffused hydrogen by virtue of homogenization during off-cycles. It is quite believable that these cycles, plus repeated erosion (washing) of the engine components plays an important role in engine reliability.



Stress corrosion characteristics can depend on alloy processing and heat treatment, and can vary from heat to heat. There appears little difference between mill-annealing and duplex-annealing bar of Ti-6Al-2Sn-4Zr-2Mo (compare Figures 2.0324 and 2.0325). Heavy forging can reduce the embrittlement and cracking thresholds, as shown in Figure 2.0326. The cracking thresholds reported for sheet and 100-hour exposure in Figures 2.0321 and 2.0322 are generally lower than those reported for bar in Figures 2.0324 through 2.0326 and the compressor wheel forging in Table 2.03211, but no worse than the compressor disc forging in Figure 2.03212.

No stress corrosion experiments have been conducted wherein salt-free specimens were exposed to the same high stresses and temperatures producing embrittlement or cracking in salt-coated specimens. In this regard, the representations of Figures 2.0327 through 2.03210 are of unusual interest. Associated with the exposure of salt-coated specimens to elevated temperatures under load, are changes in residual room temperature properties not unlike those one might expect from accelerated aging under stress. It would be interesting, indeed, to perform such salt-free specimen tests. Interesting, too, would be the use of more sensitive indicators of embrittlement such as sustained-load notch tests.

It is clear from the foregoing that no single hot-salt stress corrosion threshold can be claimed for this alloy. Material condition, exposure variables, post-exposure test procedure and corrosion criteria are all influential and account for the variability of results from one investigator to another. Some insights have been gained in resolving the disparity between laboratory and service experiences, but no assurance can be given that increased service requirements might not one day penetrate the region of unsafe combinations of stress, temperature and time in salt environments.

Results for resistance spot-welded sheet in Figure 4.0310 show freedom from salt corrosion cracking within the range of exposure conditions investigated. Significant loss of strength accompanies the elevated temperature exposure above 600 F. The influence of an 1100 F, 2-hour post-weld treatment is to reduce initial cross tension strength but minimize further reduction when exposure to elevated temperature.

### 4.03 Welding

4.031 General. Two phase, alpha-beta titanium alloys characteristically produce welds with properties different from those of the unwelded metal. Generally, as the amount of stable beta phase increases, weld strength increases and ductility and toughness decrease. The normal annealed microstructure of Ti-6Al-2Sn-4Zr-2Mo alloy is alpha phase with a small amount of beta phase. It would be expected that, with such slight beta stabilization, the alloy would have excellent weldability. This, however, is not the case. The alloy produces a weld, the properties of which are difficult to predict and markedly different from the unwelded metal (30).

Weld properties for this alloy have been reported in References 10, 13, 27 and 30. The results are summarized here. Sizable differences have been observed in room temperature strength and notch toughness for fusion welded sheet of two thicknesses from separate heats (Figure 4.036 and Table 4.037). Post-weld heat treatment in the stress relief range (i.e., 1100 F, 2 hours) aged the welds, increasing their strength and reducing their notch toughness. In contrast to the results of Figure 4.036 and Table 4.037, Figure 4.0318 and 4.0319 show essentially no differences in ordinary tensile properties for welds of two different sheet gages from a single heat, and that possible benefits may derive for post-weld annealing (this is discussed in greater length in Section 4.035). By comparison with creep data for parent sheet in Tables 3.0424 and 3.0425, poor creep resistance is seen for welded sheet in Table 4.038. Some evidence of thermal instability is also seen for welded sheet in Table 4.038. Post-weld heat treatment at 1100 F for 2 hours (stress relief cycle) improves creep resistance but not elevated temperature stability (also shown in Table 4.038). The elevated temperature weld strengths reported in Table 4.039 represent 100 percent weld efficiencies. Room temperature strength of unexposed, as-welded spot welds presented in Figure 4.0310 and Table 4.0311 are considered "satisfactory" by the alloy producer (30). Post-weld heat treatment (1100 F, 2 hours) and elevated temperature exposure in air or salt environments, however, have deleterious effects on spot weld strength (aging see Figure 4.0310).

It has been suggested (27, 30) that the variable weld properties of this alloy are attributed to nonequilibrium transformations, which accompany weld cooling. Series of tests designed to study the influences of weld cooling rate (varied by altering sheet thickness, welding speed and copper chill fixturing), base metal composition and heat treated condition on structure and properties have been conducted (27, 30). Results of bend tests in Table 4.0312, though sometimes not very pronounced, are consistent in suggesting that for this alloy the weld properties may vary with ingot source (composition), gage of material welded, weld cooling rate and, possibly, prior heat treatment. Variability of weld properties may be explained in part by reference to the transformation kinetics of this alloy as follows.



4.032 Non-equilibrium beta phase decomposition. In titanium alloys, which are allotropic, there occur non-equilibrium transformations during relatively rapid cooling (or heating). The familiar time-temperature-transformation (TTT) diagram is frequently used for the study of such nonequilibrium transformations. However, the TTT diagram is usually determined by isothermal quench techniques, and can only be used as a guide to the phase changes which take place during continuous cooling: the continuous-cooling-transformation (CCT) diagram is necessary for this purpose. As the name implies, the CCT diagram describes the phase changes that take place while the metal is cooling, as a function of its cooling rate.

A schematic CCT diagram for alpha-lean beta titanium alloys is shown in Figure 4.0313. Curve A represents very slow cooling with transformation occurring in the neighborhood of the beta transus. The reaction is a near-equilibrium transformation of beta to alpha platelets. Cooling indicated by Curve B is more rapid than that represented by Curve A (but still slow) and results in an alpha structure more serrated in nature. Cooling indicated by Curve C produces fine (acicular) alpha, but insufficient time in the upper range of temperature prevents the completion of this transformation. Some beta thus transforms to martensite (a) at the lower temperature. This split transformation, a non-equilibrium beta phase decomposition, occurs between rates  $ C_{1} $ and  $ C_{2} $. Variable cooling rates within this range result in variable transformations believed to cause many structural and mechanical uncertainties in Ti-6Al-2Sn-4Zr-2Mo welds. At sufficiently fast cooling rates, as indicated by Curve D, transformation does not occur until a low temperature is reached. Here a uniform fully martensitic structure is obtained.

4.033 Mechanical properties. Based on the CCT diagram, maximum hardness for this alloy would be expected for intermediate cooling rates. Faster rates would not permit transformation to alpha, but rather martensite, which in titanium is usually considered a soft, ductile structure. Slower rates would result

in alpha precipitates increasingly massive, soft and ductile, the slower the cooling rate.

Hardness measurements have been made on specimens cooled at various rates from above the beta transus. At slow cooling rates, transformation takes place at high temperatures and approaches equilibrium. The alpha growth rate exceeds the nucleation rate and the resultant strength is relatively soft. As the cooling rate is increased from the slowest rate, hardness increases as alpha platelet size diminishes. The nucleation rate for alpha transformation exceeds the growth rate. Hardness continues to increase with cooling rate as the "knee" of the curve is approached and the region of precipitation from an unstable beta is entered. (In actual welding situations the heat-affected zone may be further complicated by an incomplete transformation upon heating due to the short times involved – see the CCT diagram for this alloy in Figure 4.0315.) To the left of the "knee", martensite is formed, characterized by a criss-cross needle appearance. Titanium martensite is usually soft, and therefore the hardness would be expected to fall for cooling rates to the left of the "knee." However, for this alloy it does not. One authority (35) feels that this is to be expected for stock cooled from above the beta transus. The "soft" martensite is produced by quenching from below the beta transus. These two martensite differ in hardness, lattice parameters and transformation mechanism. These hardness observations are consistent with hardness surveys made on weldments, a typical result shown in Figure 4.0314. The cooling rate increases with distance from the weld centerline, and so does the hardness before it falls to the base plate value. There is no drop in the heat-affected zone to a value less than the base plate value as would be expected were the martensite in this region "soft." The full range of mechanical properties as a function of cooling rate for this alloy has not yet been developed.

Composition. The nominal CCT diagram for this alloy is shown in Figure 4.0315. Figure 4.0316 shows that variations in chemical composition within normal specification tolerances can cause significant changes in the position of the CCT diagram curves (i.e.,  $ M_{s} $,  $ M_{f} $ and times to start and finish transformations). While the differences shown may appear small, they are of considerable practical significance. A cooling rate considered slow enough to miss the region of non-equilibrium beta phase decomposition for one heat may, in fact, not be slow enough to miss it due to the curves' shift with slight chemistry changes. The heat-affected zone, which cools at quite variable rate, can be expected to consist of an equally broad range of microstructure and consequent mechanical properties.

The CCT diagram for this alloy is positioned to

the right and below the TTT diagram (see Figure 4.0317). This is to be expected since the CCT diagram is essentially non-equilibrium and the TTT diagram is pseudo-equilibrium. The time required to start the beta-to-alpha transformation is greater at higher temperatures. At a given transformation temperatures, the continuously cooled specimen has been above the temperature throughout its entire cooling cycle, whereas the isothermally treated sample has been at the given reaction temperature for a finite time. This, the continuously cooled sample is not ready to form alpha at this given temperature in the time interval the isothermally treated sample is; that is, more time is required for transformation to begin. This explains why the CCT diagram lies below and to the right of the TTT diagram. It is a more difficult and abstract case to rationalize the discrepancy in  $ M_{s} $ temperatures.

The transformation kinetics of martensite formation are different from those of the beta-to-alpha reaction. The former is by a shear mechanism (which defines martensite) and results in a supersaturated microstructure; the latter by a nucleation and growth process. It has been shown (31, 32) that the  $ M_{s} $ temperature for titanium alloys is virtually independent of cooling rate at rates faster than critical (i.e.,  $ C_{1} $ in Figure 4.0313). Thus, the  $ M_{s} $ temperature determined from either isothermal quenching or continuous cooling procedures should be the same. However, the CCT diagram shows an  $ M_{s} $ temperature of about 1300 F; the TTT diagram, 1475 F. Two explanations for this disagreement have been offered.

Armitage (33) suggests that martensite may form isothermally above the apparent  $ M_{s} $ temperature. The possible formation of small-dispersed particles of omega phase (even after cold water quenching) would produce zones of transitory enriched beta. This segregation of beta stabilizing solute elements (here molybdenum) could hen promote isothermal martensite transformation by increasing  $ M_{s} $ temperature of small solute depleted regions.

Another source (30) suggests that heat to heat differences might also contribute to  $ M_s $ differences. A variation of 1 weight percent molybdenum can depress the  $ M_s $ of the Ti-Mo binary by about 50 F in the range of 2 to 3 weight percent molybdenum (31, 32, 34). Conceivably, the combined effects of compositional variations in Ti-6Al-2Sn-4Zr-2Mo might account for the observed difference in  $ M_s $ temperature for the two transformation diagrams.

Post-weld annealing. The martensite formed by rapid cooling is unstable and reverts to alpha and beta upon re-heating. If the reheat temperature is relatively low, the weld "age hardens". At higher reheat temperatures, the weld equilibrates and

softens – commonly called annealing.

The variable structure (of variable hardness) produced at slower cooling rates is unstable due to the retention of unstable beta and ages when reheated to moderate temperatures. Reheating to sufficiently high temperatures, on the other hand, causes some of the alpha products producing hardening during cooling to be partially taken into solution. Others coalesce, equilibrium is approached and general softening occurs – this aging is called annealing.

The large alpha plates formed at cooling rates slower than those in the unstable transformation range is a “soft structure” – reheating to moderate temperatures has little effect. At higher reheat temperatures, in the annealing range, some hardening may occur due to re-distribution of elements between the alpha and beta phases.

The effects of post-weld annealing temperature and time on the room temperature tensile properties of duplex-annealed 0.025-inch and 0.080-inch sheet are shown in Figure 4.0318 and 4.0319. The welds in the 0.025-onch sheet were thought (30) to have quenched to a martensitic (alpha prime) structure; welds in the 0.080-inch sheet cooled at a lower rate producing an "indefinite" structure. Both gages exhibited essentially identical properties in the as-welded condition. Structural differences, however, account for quite different property trends with time at the annealing temperature for the two gages. It appears, nevertheless, that both sheet aged at all annealing temperatures. Overaging (or annealing) occurred, however, in a very short time at the higher temperatures.

The effect of 150-hour exposure at 1000 F without load on the subsequent room temperature tensile properties of both sheet gages annealed for 2 hours over the same range of annealing temperature is shown in Table 4.0320. It is not surprising that the instability shown was not fully eliminated by annealing even at 1400 F for 2 hours, since, as shown in Figures 4.0318 and 4.0319, four hours are required for the overaged properties of return to the unaged values. That some thermal instability might not be observed for fully annealed stock similarly exposure has not been demonstrated. It should be cautioned, however, that the specimen type used for this study (longitudinal weld orientation) tends to integrate the properties of the weld fusion and heat affected zones as well as the unwelded sheet and the possibility that the base metal was controlling the tensile properties of the exposed specimens cannot be ruled out.

While the data are incomplete, it appears that post-weld annealing may be a useful method of approaching uniform weld properties in an alloy that has demonstrated wide variability. A cycle of 1300 to 1400 F for at least one hour seems indicated. This tends to equilibrate the weld; however, its effect on base metal properties is unknown. Undoubtedly, the advantages gained by the 1100 F part of the triplex anneal would be lost.
